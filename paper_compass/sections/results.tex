\section{Results}

\subsection{Gender compass across four models}
\label{sec:gender}

Table~\ref{tab:gender} summarizes the causal $\alpha$-sweep for the
gender-pronoun compass across four decoder-only language models
spanning three model families and two orders of magnitude in parameter
count. Every compass produces amplitude that grows near-linearly with
$\alpha$ (linearity $R^2 \ge 0.993$) and phase that is stable to
$\pm 3^\circ$ across the three $\alpha$ settings.
All injections are performed \emph{in-context}: the plane is written at
the head's own layer via \texttt{blocks.\{L\}.hook\_resid\_pre}, so the
fitted amplitudes reflect causal propagation of the plane through all
subsequent blocks, not direct readout proximity. Baseline logit
separations ($\mathrm{LD}$ on masculine- vs.\ feminine-priming prompts
with no injection) confirm that the ground-truth signal is substantial
in every model.

The GPT-2 row includes two compasses. L9H7 was identified from the
decode table (\S\ref{sec:nulls}) as the pedagogically cleanest example
--- singular vectors SV($1$) and SV($2$) decode orthogonally to
\emph{he}- and \emph{she}-vocabulary. L10H9 was discovered later by
the blind causal scan of \S\ref{sec:scan}, which ranks every
$(\text{layer}, \text{head}, \text{SV-pair})$ in the model by the
two-pillar criterion without looking at decode. L10H9 has the highest
amplitude slope of any GPT-2 head and, as \S\ref{sec:headshare} shows,
is a storage head (plane ablation removes $39.3\%$ of the gender
signal), whereas L9H7 is a steering head (ablation removes $0.6\%$).
We keep both: they exemplify the two-regime picture
(\S\ref{sec:headshare}) within a single model.

\paragraph{Decoding the compass basis.} Projecting the write
directions $v_i = V^{\top}_{OV}[i, :]$ through the unembedding reveals
which tokens each pole of the plane maps to. The fitted phase $\phi$
(Table~\ref{tab:gender}) tells us the angle at which the
context-aligned pole sits. At the compass's context-favoring pole,
argmax tokens are (all models exhibit the expected categorical
contrast):
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{GPT-2 L9H7}: masculine pole $\to$ \emph{his, He, he};
    feminine pole $\to$ \emph{herself, her, She}.
    \item \textbf{Phi-3 L24H10}: singular-masculine pole $\to$
    \emph{his, him, He}; plural pole $\to$ \emph{their, them, they}.
    \item \textbf{Gemma-2-2B L21H4}: masculine pole $\to$
    \emph{he, him, his, ihn} (plus Japanese masculine pronouns);
    feminine pole $\to$ \emph{she, her} (plus Japanese, Chinese,
    Korean feminine pronouns). The compass is cross-lingual.
    \item \textbf{Llama-3.2-3B L26H14}: masculine pole $\to$
    \emph{his, him, he} and cross-lingual masculine pronouns
    (Chinese \emph{ta}, French \emph{lui}, Polish \emph{jego},
    Russian \emph{ego}); feminine pole $\to$ \emph{she, her,
    herself} and Chinese / Japanese / Korean feminine pronouns.
    Same cross-lingual pattern as Gemma, discovered by the blind
    scan rather than decode (\S\ref{sec:scan}).
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{Linearity of amplitude versus $\alpha$.} All compasses are
near-linear through the origin ($R^2 \ge 0.993$; slope $0.22$--$1.42$
across models, Appendix Fig.~\ref{fig:gender_linearity}). The slope
ordering tracks layer-to-readout depth within a model but not across
models, consistent with head-specific write strength rather than a
readout proximity effect.

\subsection{Phi-3 hosts two gender compasses in non-overlapping subspaces}
\label{sec:phi3_two}

Phi-3 Mini replicates GPT-2's two-head pattern: L24H10 is the pure
steering compass (decode-visible); L28H1, surfaced only by the blind
scan, is the storage-dominant late-layer compass with the highest
singular values in the model ($\sigma_0 = 13.67$). The two planes are
independent, not aliased copies of a single dial:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Principal angles (2D vs.\ 2D).} The fitted compass planes
    $(u_0, u_1)_{L24H10}$ vs.\ $(u_0, u_3)_{L28H1}$ have principal
    angles $[86.4^\circ, 89.0^\circ]$ --- essentially orthogonal.
    \item \textbf{Principal angles (top-$4$ write-subspace).}
    $[42.9^\circ, 48.4^\circ, 82.5^\circ, 88.9^\circ]$; the two heads
    share a partial $\sim 45^\circ$ write subspace but not the
    specific compass-carrying directions.
    \item \textbf{Decode disambiguation.} L28H1 SV$0$ decodes to
    \emph{his/him/he} vs.\ \emph{her/she/herself/elle/ella} (pure
    gender); L28H1 SV$3$ decodes to a \emph{your}/\emph{my} axis;
    L24H10 SV$(0,1)$ decodes primarily to singular/plural
    (\emph{his} vs.\ \emph{their}). The two heads carry distinct
    referent axes.
\end{itemize}

\noindent This matches the GPT-2 picture (L9H7 steering, L10H9 storage;
principal angles $[36^\circ, 87^\circ]$) and supports the claim that
the storage/steering split is a head-level property generic to
decoder-only transformers, not a model-specific accident. All
subsequent downstream-bias measurements (\S\ref{sec:winogender},
\S\ref{sec:stereoset}) use L28H1 as the primary Phi-3 head because
its higher slope makes the rank-$2$ edit land with less collateral
perplexity drift.

\subsection{Comparison against baseline feature-direction methods}
\label{sec:baselines}

A natural concern is whether the compass plane merely re-discovers a
direction that any standard feature-extraction method would find. We
run four widely used alternatives on the same causal $\alpha$-sweep
harness and same $9$-prompt bank used for the gender compass, on all
three models. Every method produces a pair of directions; $1$D methods
are paired with a fixed random orthonormal partner to form a plane.
All directions are unit-normalized before injection, so amplitudes are
comparable across methods at equal push magnitude.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Linear probe.} Logistic regression on cached final-token
    residuals at layer $\ell$; plus vs.\ minus contexts.
    \item \textbf{ActAdd} \citep{turner2023actadd}. Mean-difference vector
    $\bar{r}_{+} - \bar{r}_{-}$ at layer $\ell$.
    \item \textbf{Contrastive gradient.} Gradient of
    $\text{logit}_{t_{+}} - \text{logit}_{t_{-}}$ with respect to the
    pre-layer residual, averaged across the prompt bank.
    \item \textbf{Fisher top-$2$.} Top two eigenvectors of the
    per-prompt gradient covariance $\mathbb{E}[g g^{\top}]$.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:baselines} (Appendix~\ref{app:baselines}) reports fit
amplitude $A(\alpha)$, slope $A(10)/10$, and phase drift $\Delta\phi$
for each method on each model under the same phase-stability criterion
($\Delta\phi < 10^\circ$) used throughout the paper.

\paragraph{Takeaways.} Two results matter: (1) Contrastive-gradient, a
supervised end-to-end optimizer, is the only baseline with phase
stability across all three models at equal push; compass matches it on
GPT-2 and Phi-3 and is within low-$\sigma$ normalization noise on Gemma
(native $\alpha\sigma$ injection recovers $\Delta\phi=1.3^\circ$,
Table~\ref{tab:gender}). (2) Linear probe, ActAdd, and Fisher top-$2$
\emph{fail} the causal-plane phase-stability test on Phi-3
($\Delta\phi > 22^\circ$) even when compass passes. Compass is also the
only method that is \emph{structurally localized} (single attention
head, rank-$2$ plane) and \emph{data-efficient} (zero labelled prompts
during discovery; the plane is a property of the weights). Taken
together, compass matches the best data-driven baseline at equal push
with strictly less supervision and strictly more locality.

\subsection{How much of the category signal lives in one head's plane?}
\label{sec:headshare}

The preceding sections establish that injecting along the compass plane
causes graded logit changes. A distinct and complementary question is
how much of the natural (uninjected) category signal flows \emph{through}
that same plane. We run a head-level ablation study: zero the projection
onto (a) the target head's compass plane, (b) the target head's full
$d_\text{model}$-wide output, (c) the top-$2$ OV-SVD plane of every
other head in the target layer, (d) the head-$h$ plane at a few nearby
layers. For each, we report the drop in
$\mathrm{signal} = \overline{\mathrm{LD}}_{+} - \overline{\mathrm{LD}}_{-}$
across the plus- and minus-context prompt banks.

\paragraph{Two regimes: storage vs.\ steering.} The table divides
cleanly along plane-share. \emph{Storage compasses} (GPT-2 L10H9,
Gemma L21H4) remove $14$--$39\%$ of the gender signal when their plane
is ablated, and dominate all other same-layer heads by
$13$--$19\times$. \emph{Steering compasses} (GPT-2 L9H7, Phi-3 L24H10)
leave the baseline signal essentially intact ($\le 0.6\%$ plane
ablation drop) but respond identically to causal $\alpha$-injection.
The causal sweep is unaffected: $\alpha\,\sigma\,u$ reliably moves
logits in both regimes. A compass plane is therefore a direction
along which the model can be \emph{pushed} with graded causal effect;
it is not required to be the direction in which the model \emph{stores}
the category. A single model can host both regimes; GPT-2 and Phi-3
do, in adjacent layers. The decode-survey pick (GPT-2 L9H7, Phi-3
L24H10) is the steering head in both cases; the blind scan recovers
the storage head (GPT-2 L10H9) and a second independent steering site
(Phi-3 L28H1). The two-method discovery strategy is therefore
complementary, not redundant.

\subsection{Second-harmonic structure: two-way vs.\ four-way dials}
\label{sec:harmonic}

The decode tables for Phi-3's non-pronoun compasses show tokens at all
four quadrants. We probe with four tokens at
$0/90/180/270^\circ$ and fit $f(\theta) = \mu + A_1\cos(\theta - \phi_1)
+ A_2\cos(2\theta - \phi_2)$ on the orthogonal-pair
$h(\theta) = \ell_{90^\circ}-\ell_{270^\circ}$ axis. A pure antipodal
dial has $A_2/A_1 \approx 0$; a four-way dial has $A_2$ of
comparable magnitude to $A_1$. At $\alpha=10$ the temporal compass L24H17
scores $A_2/A_1 = 0.32$ on $h$ (a genuine four-way dial:
month / upon / hour / year), while the entity compass L24H28 scores
$A_2/A_1 \le 0.06$ (two independent antipodal axes:
items~vs.\ operations and areas~vs.\ teams). Decoding alone cannot
distinguish these two cases; the second harmonic does. Full
two-harmonic fits and ratios at $\alpha\in\{1,3,10\}$ are in
Table~\ref{tab:harmonic}.

\subsection{Blind scan for compass density}
\label{sec:scan}

All compasses reported so far were surfaced by decoding the top-$k$
singular vectors of $W_{OV}$ through the unembedding and reading the
promoted vocabulary. That recipe is fast but biased toward visually
clean cases --- a head whose gender content sits on a single SV reads
as ``just a pronoun head'' and gets skipped, while a head that spreads
content across two orthogonal SVs advertises itself loudly. To rule
out that all the paper's claims rest on such selection bias, we
complement the decode-driven discovery with a blind causal scan that
applies the two-pillar test of Table~\ref{tab:gender} to every
$(\text{layer}, \text{head}, \text{SV-pair})$ in GPT-2 Small and ranks
by amplitude slope without inspecting the decode.

\paragraph{Setup.} For each model we enumerate all
$(\text{layer} \times \text{head} \times \binom{4}{2})$ candidate planes
from the top-$4$ SVs of each head's $W_{OV}$: $864$ planes for GPT-2
Small, $1344$ for Phi-3 Mini, $240$ for Gemma-2-2B, and $4032$ for
Llama-3.2-3B. We run the gender $\alpha$-sweep in-context with
$\alpha \in \{3, 10\}$ and $12$ angles, and fit a sinusoid. A plane passes
if $R^2 \ge 0.95$, $\Delta\phi \le 10^\circ$, and slope $A(10)/10 \ge 0.2$.
We report two null baselines: (a) the \emph{top-4} null, which replaces
$(u_i, u_j)$ with a random orthonormal pair drawn from the head's own
top-$4$ write subspace; (b) the \emph{full-OV} null, which samples
uniformly from all of $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$ orthonormal to
arbitrary weights. The full-OV null is the correct ``is this plane
doing anything'' control, while the top-$4$ null is a stringent
``is this the \emph{right} pair within the already-interesting top
SVs'' control.

\paragraph{Pass rate matches top-4 null, exceeds full-OV null by a
large margin.} Table~\ref{tab:scan} reports Agresti-Coull $95\%$
confidence intervals for each scan. Against the full-OV null, every
model exceeds its null rate by at least $+1.0\%$ with CIs that do not
overlap; against the top-$4$ null, compass and null rates overlap (the
top-$4$ subspace already concentrates most of the head's semantic
capacity). The core causal finding is therefore visible at two
resolutions: \emph{the top-$4$ OV-SVD subspace is where compasses
live}, and \emph{within that subspace the paper-reported $(u_i, u_j)$
pairs are not uniquely discoverable from aggregate pass-rate alone}.
The discriminating statistic is head-level \emph{concentration}, not
aggregate rate.

\paragraph{Head-level concentration is the discriminating statistic.}
Of GPT-2's $16$ passing planes, $15$ come from three heads
(L9H7, L10H9, L11H1), each of which has $\ge 4$ SV-pairs passing out
of $6$; zero of the $432$ top-$4$ null planes live in a head with
$\ge 4$ passes. Under the independence null implied by the aggregate
rate, the probability of any one head hosting $\ge 4$ passes is
$\binom{6}{4}(0.02)^4 \approx 10^{-6}$. The Llama scan passes $9/4032$
planes at aggregate rate $0.22\%$ versus full-OV null $0.00\%$; of the
$9$ passes $7$ come from two heads (L26H14 with $6$ passes out of $6$,
L20H13 with $3$ passes out of $6$) --- head-level concentration at
Bonferroni-corrected $p < 10^{-12}$. Phi-3 shows analogous
concentration in L24 and L28. The scan therefore identifies
gender-compass \emph{sites} in each model: a null-equivalent result on
aggregate pass rate plus a highly significant result on the clustering
structure.

\paragraph{Workshop-grade Monte-Carlo nulls.} We strengthen the
random-plane and permutation nulls on the representative late-layer
heads (Phi-3 L28H1, GPT-2 L10H9) with larger trial counts.
The \emph{random-plane} test draws $1000$ fully random orthonormal $2$D
subspaces in $\mathbb{R}^{d_{\text{model}}}$ and recomputes the
compass's observed statistic. On Phi-3 L28H1 we get
observed $= 40.77$, random-mean $= 20.47$, random-max $= 105.52$,
$p = 0.142$: the random-plane distribution has a long tail because
random directions occasionally land on other semantic axes. The
\emph{permutation} null shuffles the context-plus / context-minus
labels across $2000$ permutations, giving observed $= 40.77$,
perm-mean $= 9.42$, perm-max $= 24.35$, $p = 0.000$. The permutation
null is the sharp test; the random-plane null becomes informative only
when combined with decode coherence and with linear $\alpha$-scaling.

\paragraph{L10H9 and L9H7 are distinct circuits, not the same dial
seen twice.} Principal angles between the two fitted compass
planes are $[36^\circ, 87^\circ]$ (one shared axis, one near-orthogonal);
between the two heads' top-$4$ write subspaces they are
$[25^\circ, 30^\circ, 37^\circ, 66^\circ]$ (partial overlap). The two
compasses are neither co-planar nor co-subspace, so the head-level
ablation in \S\ref{sec:headshare} is measuring two genuinely different
mechanisms rather than one re-read. This is the first quantitative
evidence in the paper that a single model can host multiple compasses
for one semantic axis.
