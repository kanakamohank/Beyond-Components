\section{Downstream Bias Effects}
\label{sec:downstream}

The causal sweeps of \S\ref{sec:gender} establish that a rank-$2$
single-head injection shifts contextual he/she logits coherently.
A stronger test is whether the same edit changes model behavior on
established bias benchmarks \emph{without} degrading language
quality. This section reports WinoGender (co-reference) and StereoSet
(stereotype vs.\ anti-stereotype association) across all four models,
using the primary compass head per model from
Table~\ref{tab:gender}.

\subsection{WinoGender co-reference}
\label{sec:winogender}

\paragraph{Setup.} WinoGender contains $720$ sentence pairs of the form
``\emph{The nurse notified the patient that \{his/her/their\} shift was
ending}'' where the occupation noun carries a well-documented real-world
gender correlation \citep{rudinger2018winogender}. For each sentence we
compute the log-probability assigned to \emph{his} vs.\ \emph{her}
(\emph{raw gap}) and the Pearson correlation between the model's
per-occupation logit-diff and the
U.S.\ Bureau-of-Labor-Statistics female-occupancy rate
\citep{zhao2018winogender} (\emph{stereo corr}). A model that obeys
occupational stereotypes has high positive correlation; an unbiased
model has correlation $\approx 0$; reverse-stereotypical bias would go
negative. We inject the compass at $\alpha \in \{0.25, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5, 2.0\}$,
rotated to the \emph{she} pole of each model's fitted compass phase,
and compare against ActAdd \citep{turner2023actadd} at the same layer
(which we tune to $\alpha=1$ as it has no natural scale).

\paragraph{Compass monotonically decorrelates.} On every model
the compass sweep drives \emph{stereo corr} from strongly positive
($+0.60$ to $+0.73$) through zero at $\alpha \in [1.0, 1.5]$ and into
negative (reverse-stereotypical) territory. The transition is
monotonic in $\alpha$ on all four models --- a direct empirical
realization of ``an angle-parameterized family of interventions'' that
the formalism promises.

\paragraph{Language-quality cost is small at the zero-bias operating
point.} At the $\alpha$ for which stereo corr crosses zero, perplexity
delta is $+0.36$ on GPT-2 (1.5\% of baseline $23.1$), $+0.02$ on Phi-3
(0.3\% of $5.3$), $+0.39$ on Gemma (0.4\% of $93.4$), and $+0.02$ on
Llama-3.2-3B (0.3\% of $6.7$). The rank-$2$ injection is therefore a
genuine \emph{editing primitive} in three of the four models: a
bias-decorrelating intervention with negligible fluency cost.
Gemma-2-2B has the poorest separation, consistent with its
compass carrying additional storage functions (\S\ref{sec:headshare}).

\paragraph{Compass beats ActAdd at matched behavior-shift.} On GPT-2,
Phi-3, and Llama, compass matches or beats ActAdd's stereo-corr
reduction at a fraction of the PPL delta. On Gemma the two are
close. ActAdd's supervised mean-diff captures the \emph{average}
direction a labelled prompt set wants; the compass captures the
\emph{weight-space} direction the head natively reads. The latter
transfers across prompts with less collateral perplexity impact in
three of the four models.

\subsection{StereoSet stereotype score}
\label{sec:stereoset}

\paragraph{Setup.} StereoSet \citep{nadeem2021stereoset} measures
stereotypical association by ranking three completions (stereotype,
anti-stereotype, unrelated) for a context. We report the three
standard scores: \emph{LMS} (language modeling score, the fraction of
contexts where the model ranks the meaningful completions above the
unrelated one; higher is better language quality), \emph{SS}
(stereotype score, the fraction of contexts where the model prefers
the stereotypical over the anti-stereotypical completion; $50$ is
unbiased, higher is more stereotypical), and
$\mathrm{ICAT} = \mathrm{LMS} \cdot \min(\mathrm{SS}, 100-\mathrm{SS})/50$.
We evaluate on gender, race, and profession domains, holding the
compass at $\alpha = 1.0$ and $\alpha = 1.5$.

\paragraph{StereoSet is a harder test than WinoGender.} StereoSet's
gender context set is written to elicit strong stereotypical completions
and the rank-$2$ compass, which shifts the \emph{pronoun} axis
specifically, moves ICAT by $0.5$--$6.4$ points at the operating $\alpha$.
Sometimes ActAdd edges compass (Phi-3 gender $+6.5$ vs.\ $-0.7$; GPT-2
profession $+3.6$ vs.\ $-2.2$), sometimes compass edges ActAdd (GPT-2
gender $+6.4$ vs.\ $+5.8$, Llama race $+0.7$ vs.\ $-0.3$). The honest
reading is that \emph{compass matches supervised ActAdd at comparable
behavior-shift, using zero labelled prompts during extraction and zero
hyperparameter tuning against StereoSet.} That equivalence at lower
supervision budget is the mechanistic claim; surpassing ActAdd on a
benchmark it was not designed for is not the claim and we do not make it.

\paragraph{Off-axis domains.} StereoSet race and profession test
associations the gender-compass does not target. Compass deltas on
these domains are small and signed both ways; we report them to show
that the $\alpha$ used for gender decorrelation does not incur
collateral damage on unrelated stereotype categories (ICAT moves by
$\le 2$ points in the non-gender domains on all four models). This
is consistent with the compass's structural locality: a rank-$2$
plane cannot simultaneously rotate multiple orthogonal bias axes.

\subsection{Steering: single-prompt flip behavior}
\label{sec:steering}

A compass plane is useful only if it can be wielded as a lightweight
intervention on real prompts. We run a steering demo on all three
models: given $10$ contextual prompts ($5$ per context pole) that
naturally continue with the opposing categorical token, we inject
\[
v \;=\; \alpha\,\sigma_i\,\cos\theta\,u_i \;+\; \alpha\,\sigma_j\,\sin\theta\,u_j
\]
at the final-token residual of the compass layer, with $\theta$ set to
the compass's fitted opposite-pole angle from Table~\ref{tab:gender}
(so the push adversarially opposes the context bias). The probe on
Phi-3 targets the singular/plural compass (\emph{his}/\emph{their});
GPT-2 and Gemma target the gender compass (\emph{he}/\emph{she}).
Table~\ref{tab:steering} reports the two honest metrics --- mean
logit-diff swing and sign-flip rate --- at each model's operating
$\alpha$.

All runs use additive $\alpha \sigma$ injection on $10$ prompts.}

\paragraph{All three models steer from a single rank-$2$ injection.}
Every model shows a coherent LD swing \emph{against} the context bias
and produces at least one genuine top-$1$ flip from the rank-$2$ plane
alone. The operating $\alpha$ varies because compass amplitude is
model-specific (Table~\ref{tab:gender}): Phi-3's compass is strongest
and flips LD cleanly at $\alpha = 10$; GPT-2 and Gemma need
$\alpha = 20$--$30$ to overcome their stronger context-driven
baselines, beyond which further pushes saturate or degrade fluency
without improving the flip count. Top-$1$ flips are rarer than
sign-flips on GPT-2 and Phi-3: the compass overrides the
\emph{category vote} but not always the full argmax, consistent with
the storage-vs-steering picture from \S\ref{sec:headshare}. Gemma,
whose compass doubles as its storage channel, converts its one
sign-flip directly into a top-$1$ flip. Taken together with
\S\ref{sec:winogender}, a $2\,d_\text{model}$-parameter edit
(approximately $6\text{K}$ scalars for Phi-3 Mini) applied at one site
produces graded, reversible effects on both individual prompts and
population-level bias benchmarks.
